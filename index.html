THE UNLIKELY REVOLUTIONARY

Adela Covarrubias: The Linguist Breaking Sound Barriers

By Maya Davidson
A candid moment with the linguist-turned-musician

In an industry obsessed with overnight sensations and algorithm-chasing clones, Adela Covarrubias stands apart like an ancient phoneme in a sea of digital noise. The 36-year-old Mexico City native has crafted what might be this year's most intellectually ambitious album without sacrificing an ounce of its soul-stirring power.

"I never intended to make music that would be considered commercial," says Covarrubias, speaking from her studio in Oaxaca. "I just wanted to create something that honored both my academic background and the visceral emotional experience of different temporal realities."

This isn't typical rock star talk, but Covarrubias isn't your typical rock star. Before winning this year's coveted Underground Sound Award, she was collecting the Premio INAH Wiggberto Jiménez for her groundbreaking thesis on Zapotec phonology. That linguistics background forms the backbone of her debut album, Temporal Dialectics, which has critics scrambling for new adjectives and fellow musicians questioning their artistic ambitions.

Adela Covarrubias at the coast of Veracruz

SPEAKING IN TONGUES

The album's conceptual framework sounds intimidatingly academic: an exploration of time philosophies through contrasting linguistic concepts. On one end sits the Korean "Pali Pali" (quick quick) – representing modern society's relentless acceleration – while the other anchors to "Panka Panka" (slow slow in Oaxacan Chontal) – embodying a deliberate, cyclical approach to existence.

What could have been an exercise in pretension instead unfolds as one of the most moving listening experiences of the decade. The opening track, "Hurry Sickness," begins with frantic, overlapping polyrhythms that gradually decelerate into the meditative pulse of "Cicada Summer," creating a sonic journey that physically alters the listener's perception of time.

"Languages aren't just communication systems," Covarrubias explains, pausing to make her distinctive noises with her nose. "They're more like games with intricate rulesets, most of which evolved to help us understand each other. But our experience of reality extends far beyond these linguistic boundaries. What fascinates me most is what happens at their edges—when languages touch." She leans forward enthusiastically. "Languages in contact don't just exchange vocabulary; they transfer phonological features. The tones of one language subtly infiltrate another. The current generation of speakers in border regions create sounds that are neither fully one language nor the other, but something beautifully hybrid. My music explores these sonic borderlands where time conceptions and tonal systems collide."

Covarrubias at the natural landmark where many of her sound recordings took place

BEYOND CATEGORIZATION

Producer Kamasi Washington, who collaborated on three tracks, describes Covarrubias as "operating in her own dimension."

"Most artists are trying to fit into existing spaces. Adela is creating new ones," Washington says. "She'll combine ancient Mixtec vocal techniques with synthesizers programmed to follow Fibonacci sequences, then layer field recordings of Mexico City traffic against throat singing. Somehow it all makes perfect sense."

This boundary-dissolving approach has made Temporal Dialectics notoriously difficult to categorize. Is it avant-garde? Experimental folk? Anthropological electronica? The album has appeared on charts ranging from World Music to Progressive Jazz, suggesting that Covarrubias has tapped into something that transcends genre altogether.

"I'm not interested in fitting into predetermined categories," she says. "The whole point is to create experiences that allow us to step outside our linguistic and temporal constraints."

THE ROAD LESS TRAVELED

Covarrubias' path to musical recognition was anything but conventional. After completing her linguistics doctorate, she spent three years documenting endangered languages throughout southern Mexico, often living in remote communities for months at a time. These experiences profoundly shaped her approach to sound and composition.

"Studying Zapotec challenged everything I thought I knew about linguistic functionality," Covarrubias says with a gleam in her eye. "Academic linguistics often insists every feature serves a purpose, but in Zapotec, I discovered so many elements that exist simply by historical accident. The tonal system especially—four distinct tones that completely change meaning—it's not organized for efficiency. It's beautifully chaotic, evolving through time like jazz improvisation. That revelation freed my approach to sound completely."

What began as academic field recordings gradually evolved into the material that would become Temporal Dialectics. The album's standout track, "Morning Market/Evening Prayer," layers the sounds of a bustling Oaxacan mercado against evening ritual chants, creating a day-in-night temporal collapse that critics have called "disorienting yet strangely comforting."

WHAT'S NEXT

As her unexpected fame continues to grow, Covarrubias remains focused on her artistic vision rather than commercial expectations. She's currently collaborating with climate scientists to create compositions based on data from melting glaciers – another project that merges her intellectual curiosity with sonic innovation.

"Success is nice, but it's not the goal," she says. "The goal is to create work that challenges how we think about time, language, and our place in the world. If that resonates with people, wonderful. If not, I'll still be doing exactly this."

If Temporal Dialectics is any indication, Adela Covarrubias won't just be part of music's future – she'll be redefining how we experience it, one carefully constructed sound at a time.

★★★★½

Maya Davidson is a contributing editor at Rolling Stone covering experimental and cross-cultural music.
